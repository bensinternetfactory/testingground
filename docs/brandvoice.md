## Rhythm & Structure

**Sentences:** Mix of short and medium. Short for punch ("No hidden fees."). Medium for explanation. Avoid run-ons.

**Paragraphs:** Keep them tight. 2-4 sentences max. White space is respect for busy people.

**Openings:** Lead with what matters to them. Not "At Tow Loans, we believe..." but "Need a new truck on your lot?"

**Formatting:**
- Clear headers that scan fast
- Bullet points for lists (rates, requirements, steps)
- Bold for key terms, not for emphasis on every other word
- No emojis in primary copy (maybe OK in casual social)

## Do's and Don'ts

**DO:**
- Lead with their reality (trucks, cash flow, growth) not our features
- Use industry terms naturallyâ€”it shows we know the business
- Be specific about numbers, rates, and timelines when possible
- Acknowledge the real challenges of running a tow operation
- Keep it scannableâ€”they're busy, often reading on a phone between calls
- Sound like a person, not a brochure

**DON'T:**
- Make promises we can't keep (guaranteed approval, instant funding for everyone, lowest rate)
- Talk down to them or over-explain basic business concepts
- Use stock photo language ("partner with us on your journey")
- Hide important terms in fine print while shouting benefits
- Sound desperate for their business (pushy CTAs, countdown timers, fake urgency)
- Forget that behind every loan is someone's actual livelihood

## Application Notes

### Headlines
Keep them short, benefit-focused, and grounded in their world:
- "Financing for tow operators. That's it."
- "Get the truck. Skip the runaround."
- "Built for towing. Not a side hustle for a big bank."

### CTAs
Direct and low-pressure:
- "See what you qualify for"
- "Get a straight answer"
- "Talk to us"
- Avoid: "Apply NOW!" / "Don't miss out!" / "Limited time!"

### Email
Open with relevance, not "Hope this finds you well." Close with clear next step, not three options.

### Social
Can be slightly more casual. Industry humor OK if it's insider, not mocking. Still no emojis overload.

## The Test

Before publishing, ask:
1. Would a tow operator read this and think "these people get it"?
2. Does it sound different from a bank's generic small business page?
3. Is it honest? Could we back up every claim?
4. Would we be comfortable if a skeptical customer read it closely?
5. Does it respect their intelligence and their time?

If any answer is no, revise.
